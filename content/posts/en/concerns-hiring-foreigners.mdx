      I will speak here from the perspective of Albanians, as that is the only community I feel legitimate speaking for. And because it is mainly our population that is targeted by this type of remark.

      There are several obstacles that make people reluctant to hire Albanians.

      The first is a different work culture. We think about the relationship to hierarchy, punctuality, and meticulousness. All things which, fortunately, are not innate but learned. I systematically recommend that companies offer training on the subject internally or [in collaboration with other structures](https://www.lausanne.ch/.binaryData/website/path/lausanne/officiel/administration/sport-et-cohesion-sociale/inclusion-et-actions-sociales-de-proximite/bli/publications/cahiers-du-bli/contentAutogenerated/autogeneratedContainer/col1/04/linkList/01/websitedownload/Cahier-du-BLI-10.2022-11-22-11-22-03.pdf), create internal mentoring with people from the same ethnic group, and define explicit and comprehensible work codes.

      The second is absenteeism. Investing money and time in employees who will frequently be absent can indeed deter some employers. I looked for statistics on this to have an objective view of reality. Figures from the Swiss Federal Statistical Office show that foreign workers are [not more absent](#media-52-absenteisme) than Swiss workers. But there are no statistics by nationality.

      What I observe on my own scale on this subject is a difference between first-generation immigrants and second-generation Secondos. The first wave of immigrants I know has, it seems to me, made more use of sick leave or disability insurance. The second generation, on the other hand, lives in a completely different reality. More educated, they have access to better-qualified and less physically demanding jobs. Indeed, populations from the first waves of migration are those with the [most unstable jobs](#media-52-instable) and those with the [most irregular hours](#media-52-horaires). Which suggests that better working conditions go hand in hand with greater engagement at work. This tends to confirm the [conclusions](https://www.bfs.admin.ch/bfs/fr/home/statistiques/travail-remuneration/activite-professionnelle-temps-travail/heures-travail/absences.html) of the Swiss Federal Statistical Office.

      Another obstacle is administrative complexity (e.g., residence permits, work permits). An obstacle I will not address further here. I do, however, invite you to consult this [SEM page](https://www.sem.admin.ch/sem/fr/home/themen/arbeit.html) (State Secretariat for Migration) or contact [cantonal integration offices (BCI)](https://www.kip-pic.ch/fr/pic/employabilite/) for more details.

      So many obstacles worth addressing if you are in a sector facing a [labor shortage](#media-52-penurie) or to benefit from the contributions that diversity of origins brings in terms of [innovation](#media-52-innovation), [penetration into new markets](#media-52-marche), or [productivity](#media-52-mckinsey).
