      It is quite difficult for each of us to imagine being a person who might
      participate in injustices, even involuntarily. Most of us try to act with
      respect and not to cause harm around us.


      The idea of discrimination is often associated with deliberate, intentional
      negative acts: insulting others, excluding them, or denying them career
      opportunities. Yet discrimination is not limited to these visible gestures.
      It can also occur at [other levels](#media-01-Niveaux_discriminations
      "Discrimination: a multi-level system"), in a much more diffuse way and
      without any intent to harm.


      A company can, for example, have promotion criteria for leadership positions
      that are based on rather masculine qualities, such as assertiveness and
      independence, [without even realizing it](#media-01-Qualites_masculines).
      Here, the HR managers responsible for applying the procedure might not
      intend to discriminate at all, but would still end up favoring male talent.


      Another example in the field of healthcare: medical semiology (the analysis
      of symptoms to develop a diagnosis) is often conducted on white bodies. A
      physician may, for example, miss the diagnosis of [Kawasaki disease in a
      Black patient](#media-01-Kawasaki "Kawasaki disease"), because none of the
      textbooks during their studies illustrated the signs on Black bodies.


      In these situations, no one necessarily intends to cause harm, but the
      effects are very real. That is why we talk about systemic discrimination.
      It is produced by rules, habits, and frameworks that go beyond individuals
      and their intentions.


      Think of these systems of discrimination as treadmills. Even if a person
      does not move, [they will still advance](#media-01-Tapis_roulant_homme). To
      go against the treadmill, [they will have to go against the current](#media-01-Tapis_roulant_chiots).
      A person without an actively anti-racist or anti-sexist stance will not
      change the system. This system can continue to distribute advantages to
      privileged groups and obstacles to marginalized groups without any
      resistance.


      In other words, "I am clean" is often synonymous with "I do not want to
      discriminate intentionally." But to truly be clean, one would need to
      actively fight against discrimination. One would need to actively push for
      progress on the rights of transgender people, on improving accessibility
      for people with disabilities, etc.
