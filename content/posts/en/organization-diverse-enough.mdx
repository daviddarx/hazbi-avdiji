      Appearances are often deceiving in D\&I.

      First, the perception you have of inclusion, especially if you belong to a privileged group, will differ from others'. A 2020 study showed that racialized employees often have a [lower sense of inclusion](#media-26-sentiment_inclusion_racise) than white employees. The same is true in Switzerland with women who perceive the country as [more unequal](#media-26-perception_egalite) than men.

      Second, we might tell ourselves that if no problem has been reported to us, there is no problem. Yet an absence of reports often indicates a lack of trust among employees. People wonder what you will do with the report, how you will handle it, whether it will backfire on them.

      Among the other things that can escape our notice, there are also microaggressions. These are [brief and subtle remarks or behaviors](#media-26-microaggressions) that we may not even be aware of. Microaggressions may seem trivial to people who are not targeted. But people from marginalized groups experience them daily and repeatedly: this is the [erosion principle](#media-26-erosion). They show that you have biases towards others, that you see them through stereotypes. This can put others on their guard. Some microaggressions may even be intended as compliments: "I love how open-minded you are for a Muslim." These microaggressions that slip under our radar are happening all the time.

      Inclusion is also not automatic. A 2017 study of more than 500 American companies showed that moving from 30% to 50% women in teams only increases their participation in decision-making [by 1%](#media-26-ecart_inclusion). Improving diversity does not automatically translate into including these people in decision-making processes. Without active inclusion efforts, they will be pushed out of decision-making circles.

      Finally, inclusion is not just about getting along well — there are also all the policies and structures in place that will exclude certain groups, often implicitly. Is remote work granted more easily to parents than to others? Do racialized female employees face lower promotion rates than their colleagues? Do female employees have less access to [continuing education](#media-26-formation) than male employees?

      The best way to find out is to conduct an audit that will shed light on all these blind spots you may not have seen.
