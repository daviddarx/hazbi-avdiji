      Let us take the example of [progressive lenses](#media-31-lunettes). It is clear that this technological advance has improved the daily lives of a large part of humanity, to the point that people with presbyopia would not consider themselves as living with a disability.

      Medical and technological progress is indeed desirable for easing the lives of some or empowering others. Who has not been moved by videos of babies born deaf hearing for the first time thanks to [hearing devices](#media-31-baby)? Or [exoskeletons](#media-31-exosquelette) that allow people to walk?

      Where we need to start being critical of ourselves and others is when this vision takes over everything: we wait for research to do its work, because "that is the only way to help people with disabilities."

      This vision has a name: the medical model, which sees disability as a pathology to be cured and/or stigmatized. It relegates responsibility for accessibility to very specific domains (such as university hospitals and technical universities for the research and development of new assistive technologies).

      The other model, [the social model](#media-31-models), pushes us to take responsibility. What is disabling is not only the physical or psychological condition, but society and the way it has been built. This model pushes us to adopt an active posture: until technological progress materializes, there is an enormous amount we can do to improve the accessibility of our societies.

      A typical example: the medical model would advise people with attention disorders to take medication during exam periods. The social model would advise educational institutions to allow extra time for tests.

      I leave you to savor [this video](#media-31-rihanna) of a deaf mother watching Rihanna's performance at the Super Bowl in 2023, accompanied by an American Sign Language interpreter.

      To companies: it is up to you to see all the accommodations you can put in place in your organizations and thus contribute your part.
