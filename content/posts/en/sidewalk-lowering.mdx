  - content: "We would love to include everyone, but we don't have an unlimited budget. We often get the impression that there is a trade-off to be found [between inclusion and efficiency](#media-81-equilibre). Yet thinking about accessibility (1) does not always require enormous resources and (2) can have unexpected positive effects for many unsuspected people.\n\nLet's take the first point. Many accommodations are easy to implement and low-cost. We can think of remote work, which has facilitated access to employment for many people with disabilities. Or [soundproof, enclosed booths](#media-81-cabine) in open-plan offices that allow autistic people to withdraw in cases of sensory and social overstimulation.\n\nNow let's take the second point. Many objects and services developed for people with disabilities will have a snowball effect and ultimately benefit a much wider audience. This effect has a name: [the \"curb cut effect\"](#media-81-trottoir). A curb cut will benefit people in wheelchairs, as well as parents with strollers, people with luggage, or delivery workers.\n\nWe can also revisit the example of remote work: it also benefits people living in more rural areas, caregivers, and people in tighter financial situations (reducing commuting and meal costs).\n\nThere are even figures on the extent of the \"curb cut effect.\" Microsoft, for example, quantified the effect for [one-handed mode](#media-81-one_handed). People with only one hand number 26,000 in the USA. But this mode can also be useful for the 13 million people with an arm injury or for the [8 million](#media-81-microsoft) new parents who often need to hold their babies in their arms.\n\nWhat was designed for a specific group of people turns out to be useful for a population far larger than one might have imagined. Accessibility therefore also has a volume benefit.\n\nFor reference, there are 1.5 million people with disabilities in Switzerland.\n\nIt thus seems quite obvious to develop inclusive design reflexes. For this, we can reflect on all the [capabilities](#media-81-capacites) required to use a product or service. Does it require clear vision, fine dexterity, or sustained attention? And bear in mind that these capabilities can change throughout the day (some people have less concentration at the end of the day) or throughout our lives.\n\nTo summarize: accessibility is first about meeting the needs of people with disabilities, but also, by extension, simplifying life for everyone. And in some cases, even a [democratization of luxury](#media-81-luxury)?\n"
