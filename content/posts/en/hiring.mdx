      For once, I am going to tell you that I completely agree with this statement, but not for the reasons you might think. Yes, hiring someone for their identity, especially if the identity does not bring much value, is positive discrimination. But when we look at who gets a free pass because of their identity, it is [usually the same people](#media-16-Munich).

      A study commissioned by the Swiss Confederation sent CVs en masse and analyzed the number of calls received for an interview. The CVs were identical in every way (same skills, same experiences, same hobbies, even the same nationality), except for the person's name. On one side were CVs with a Swiss-sounding name (e.g., Xavier Loup) and on the other CVs with a foreign-sounding name (e.g., Hazbi Avdiji). Albanians come out the worst: we must send [44% more CVs](#media-16-CV) than Xavier Loup to land the same number of interviews.

      Another study, American this time, analyzed the chances of a female candidate being hired based on the gender of the other 3 finalists. When among the 4 final candidates there are 3 women and 1 man, a woman's chances of being hired are 67% (already there, we see a small bias since it should be 75%).

      When there are 2 men and 2 women, we fall back on parity with 50% chances. And when the female candidate is alone facing 3 men? 25%? 10%? 5%? Well no, [her chances are 0%](#media-16-HBR). All because the female candidacy will be seen as different from the others (and therefore deviant), even with equal competencies.

      [Another study](#media-16-words) showed that different words are used to evaluate male and female employees, even with equal competencies and performance. Men are attributed more positive adjectives than women. And the adjectives are different. In a man, you will see his analytical, reliable, logical, or pragmatic side. Women are seen as empathetic, organized, or enthusiastic.

      What these studies ultimately highlight is the [difference in perspectives](#media-16-visions) between what I call the benefit vision (focus on the person's strengths) and the deficit vision (focus on weaknesses and shortcomings). People belonging to privileged groups are seen through the benefit lens: they are often seen as competent and at worst given the benefit of the doubt. For marginalized people, [at the slightest mistake](#media-16-moindre_erreur), they are concluded to be neither capable nor competent.

      That is where the real favoritism lies. But more importantly, the opportunity to hire talents that others have overlooked.
