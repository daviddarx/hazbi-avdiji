      If your organization has [HR policies](#media-25-annonce) aimed at all employees that are generous, that is a very good thing.

      The problem is not there. There is a problem if you only have generalist policies and want to pass that off as diversity and inclusion. A few reasons:

      * One of the signals you send is that you do not see the specificities of each group and their particular challenges, e.g., accessibility and [workplace accommodations](#media-25-ai) for people with disabilities, language courses for people with a migration background, facilitated leave for caregivers.
      * You can also send the signal that your D\&I strategy is performative, that you are engaging in diversity-washing. Essentially, you want to show that you consider D\&I important, without actually doing it. As if you were trying to make something new out of something old, wrapping the same product in different packaging. This can be counterproductive and damage your image.
      * Most standard HR policies are modeled on the [archetype](#media-25-archetype) of a model employee. Some will benefit everyone, but many of them, without our realizing it, will only benefit part of the workforce (e.g., [expatriation support](https://www.lemonde.fr/emploi/article/2015/06/01/les-femmes-partent-peu-bien-que-les-entreprises-les-y-incitent_4644999_1698637.html) policies).

      Sometimes, we might tell ourselves that targeted policies can create tensions among people who do not benefit from them. Indeed, the risk exists and it is sometimes what makes us reluctant to implement targeted policies.

      The solution is quite simple in these cases: just take inspiration from what you did when you put in place a policy that does not benefit everyone but is not questioned (e.g., family allowances).

      And remember, there are plenty of D\&I initiatives that do not cost a fortune. It is worth trying.
