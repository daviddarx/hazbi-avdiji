      Quick exercise: close your eyes and visualize five CEOs you know, from Switzerland or elsewhere.

      Done?

      How many are women? Not many, right?

      The figures confirm it: only [22% of women](#media-44-schilling) in management positions in Switzerland in the private sector, 26% in the public sector. For a group that represents half the population, there is room for improvement.

      This is what is known as the glass ceiling: advancement becomes increasingly difficult [at each rung](#media-44-schilling2).

      And yet, even when parity is achieved, power remains filtered. When a meeting room has about one third women, they hold the floor during decision-making [one third of the time](#media-44-ecart_inclusion). When they represent half of the workforce, speaking time does not change.

      Increasing the proportion of women in leadership positions is necessary but not sufficient. It is also necessary to change corporate culture and create the right conditions for women to feel legitimate and comfortable taking the floor. Otherwise, it remains only a surface-level parity. A silent parity.

      Another thing to be mindful of is that many women will not even have the chance to feel the glass ceiling, since they cannot even get off the bottom rung. It is as if there were a [sticky floor](#media-44-plancher).

      We must therefore address the obstacles at every level, starting at the bottom.

      But one might ask: why bother?

      Because organizations where there is parity or a majority of women will make products that are more [safe](#media-44-crash) and [well-adapted](#media-44-adaptes), achieve [better financial results](#media-44-finance), etc. We all stand to gain.
