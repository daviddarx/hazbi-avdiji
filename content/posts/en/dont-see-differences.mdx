      To understand what lies behind this good intention, we need to understand the [traditional model of discrimination](#media-06-tigres). This model states that our brains would have developed shortcuts to react more quickly and thus save energy.

      These shortcuts are stereotypes that sit at the cognitive level, such as having in mind that "a tiger is dangerous" if you are on a savanna.

      These stereotypes give rise to an emotional reaction, which we call a prejudice. To continue the example, the prejudice regarding tigers would be to fear them.

      Finally, these feelings give rise to a behavior: fleeing when you see a tiger.

      To illustrate the model with an example related to diversity, let us take [homophobia](#media-06-homophobie): homosexuality is unnatural (cognitive), therefore homosexuality is disgusting (affective), and therefore homosexual people must be excluded (behavior).

      Now we can understand why some people are afraid to name differences: they conflate stereotype with prejudice. They get the impression that talking about differences will automatically lead to negative assessments.

      But this is not necessarily the case. Proof of this is that the different inclusion models we have seen [in this misconception](https://www.avdiji.com/idee-recue/cest-aux-minorites-de-sadapter) do not influence stereotypes, prejudices, and discrimination [in the same way](#media-06-modeles_stereotypes).

      It is entirely possible to have positively connoted stereotypes on the basis of negative prejudices: thinking that Chinese people are gifted in science while despising them.

      Just as it is equally possible to have negatively connoted stereotypes without having negative prejudices: acknowledging that people from non-Western migration backgrounds have less ease integrating than people from neighboring countries in order to develop targeted integration policies.

      In the end, it is difficult to design support initiatives for marginalized groups if we do not pay attention to differences and the impact they have. Treating everyone the same way does not address the specific needs of each group. It is as if we did not create [curb cuts](#media-06-abaissement_trottoir) for people in wheelchairs or parents with strollers. Universalism ultimately leads to discrimination.
