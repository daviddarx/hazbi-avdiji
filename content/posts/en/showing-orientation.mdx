      When you announce to your department that you are expecting a [happy
      event](#media-72-pregnancy), or when you show photos of your romantic
      weekend getaway, or when you and colleagues discuss which actors and
      actresses you find attractive, you are talking about sexuality at work.


      I can understand the modesty some people feel about discussing an intimate
      topic like sexuality. But what some people struggle to realize is that
      heterosexuality is everywhere, all the time, even at work. But because it
      is normalized, we do not even notice it. Like a fish that does not realize
      [the water it swims in](#media-72-poisson).


      For LGB people, talking about their relationship life is a constant
      calculation: "should I say my partner? should I go alone to the year-end
      dinner?" This is what is known as coming out: the act of announcing to the
      world that one has an orientation different from heterosexuality.


      As long as the topic is not discussed, LGB employees will resort to what is
      called covering: the act of concealing/downplaying certain aspects of their
      identity so as not to create waves among others. This technique is not
      exclusive to LGBT+ people. Many women wear [masculine-style suits](#media-72-complet)
      to remind others as little as possible that they are women, or Black women
      who [straighten their hair](#media-72-femme_noire) at work to make it look
      more like Caucasian styles. In fact, even [cisgender heterosexual white
      men](#media-72-tatouage) hide aspects of their identities, even if [to a
      lesser degree](#media-72-couverture).


      It has been shown that hiding one's identity requires so much constant
      vigilance (should I say it or not? will they notice or not?) that
      [performance
      drops](https://d1wqtxts1xzle7.cloudfront.net/73417702/bf0306105120211022-24173-9j4mg3-libre.pdf?1634935314=\&response-content-disposition=inline%3B+filename%3DSocial_identity_and_self_presentation_at.pdf\&Expires=1752752376\&Signature=fBUmrfTlv-Mi-rfZvBREED2JVyVouOXaAHPHoOIksSS7xp6oUohg~oYVRSFGZwnFkdGnsBudt1rdfkqbj1jro5~QPVcMG3uDI1bDMGNZ5I3-ORTVRPaIuB1AWQYSgCjkSLZsodIVrD6D-UsnTV1JzKeGUGHw51P4yo-nsENSyVu7n4SqrAiWBWwyGOGFss95cWiLyIKFZHTCKOlGMIDFQ5M0383z2MqsKE-27k06qsqg9jVzwU2JuHd0nOAGq0NwejmXJ34BREH22g757ZpsPFXGk8J6ZlhpvfUsz1rHovpb2ayGNkPUHbYtKK2FbLMqpd8mZkFmnYPA237oxfpGZQ__\&Key-Pair-Id=APKAJLOHF5GGSLRBV4ZA).
      Conversely, openly discussing identity differences increases job satisfaction
      among those concerned and reduces their turnover.


      Unlike other identities, LGB people will not necessarily have access to
      other LGB people within their families. Unlike gender, where one can be
      guided by a family member of the same gender, or ethnicity which is shared
      in most families, LGB people will not have many people to turn to regarding
      their sexual orientation. Hence [the importance of representation](#media-72-representation)
      of sexual and gender diversity.


      I leave the last word in English to [Chrys the Author](#media-72-chrys).
